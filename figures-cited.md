# Figures Cited in *Magnifica Humanitas*

This file provides brief context on the persons and works Pope Leo XIV explicitly cites in the encyclical. It exists so that when an audit finding references a citation in the encyclical, the agent can understand who is being cited and why. This is not a reading list of background influences — only persons actually cited by Pope Leo XIV in *Magnifica Humanitas*.

The most-cited sources are the recent papal predecessors and the Second Vatican Council; these provide the doctrinal continuity within which the encyclical operates. The non-papal citations are listed second.

## Papal predecessors and Vatican II

The encyclical situates itself as the latest contribution to a continuous tradition of Catholic Social Doctrine stretching from Leo XIII (1891) through Francis (2024). Pope Leo XIV's choice of the name "Leo XIV" is itself an evocation of Leo XIII and the inauguration of the modern social tradition.

### Leo XIII (1810–1903)

Pope from 1878 to 1903. Author of *Rerum Novarum* (1891), the founding document of modern Catholic Social Doctrine, addressing the conditions of industrial workers, the rights of labor, and the duties of capital. *Magnifica Humanitas* marks the 135th anniversary of *Rerum Novarum* and is structured as its successor for the digital age — the *res novae* of today's technological revolution being analogous to the *res novae* (new things) of the industrial revolution Leo XIII addressed.

Most-cited in *Magnifica Humanitas*: §3, §30, §175 (slavery condemnation).

### Pius XI (1857–1939)

Pope from 1922 to 1939. Author of *Quadragesimo Anno* (1931), which extended Leo XIII's social teaching, addressed totalitarian movements, and formulated the principle of subsidiarity. Cited in §31.

### Pius XII (1876–1958)

Pope from 1939 to 1958. Wartime and reconstruction pope. Coined the term "Social Doctrine of the Church" in 1950. Cited for natural-law foundation of international order and warnings against law-by-utility-or-force. §32, §219.

### John XXIII (1881–1963)

Pope from 1958 to 1963. Convened the Second Vatican Council. Author of *Mater et Magistra* and *Pacem in Terris* — the latter being the first social encyclical addressed not only to Catholics but to "all people of good will," linking dignity to human rights and proposing a direction for society based on truth, justice, love, and freedom. Cited in §33.

### Second Vatican Council (1962–1965)

Specifically *Gaudium et Spes* (1965), the pastoral constitution on the Church in the modern world, and *Dignitatis Humanae*, the declaration on religious freedom. *Gaudium et Spes* is the encyclical's most-cited single document, used architecturally throughout. The Council marks the Church's commitment to historical engagement rather than withdrawal. §20, §34, and many citations in chapters 2–3.

### Paul VI (1897–1978)

Pope from 1963 to 1978. Author of *Populorum Progressio* (1967) on integral human development as "the new name for peace," and *Octogesima Adveniens* (1971). Originator of the phrase "civilization of love." Cited frequently — especially for integral human development (§35, §82), the civilization of love (§38, §186), and the warning that "the most extraordinary scientific progress... unless accompanied by authentic moral and social progress, will in the long run go against man" (§94, §117).

### John Paul II (1920–2005)

Pope from 1978 to 2005. The most-cited predecessor in *Magnifica Humanitas*. Author of:
- *Redemptor Hominis* (1979) — source of the litmus question repeated in §129
- *Laborem Exercens* (1981) — on the dignity of work, cited extensively in Chapter 4
- *Sollicitudo Rei Socialis* (1987) — on solidarity and "structures of sin"
- *Centesimus Annus* (1991) — on the centenary of *Rerum Novarum*
- *Veritatis Splendor* (1993) — on the foundation of moral truth
- *Evangelium Vitae* (1995) — on the right to life

Wojtyła's personalism — the foundation of his theology — is operative throughout the encyclical's anthropological claims, especially on dignity (§51) and the personalistic norm against using persons as means (§53). The dignity-as-infinite framing comes from his Angelus address of 1980, cited in §53.

### Benedict XVI (1927–2022)

Pope from 2005 to 2013. Author of *Caritas in Veritate* (2009), the encyclical that placed charity at the heart of social doctrine and addressed globalization, finance, and integral development. Cited in §5, §40–41, §75–76 (intergenerational justice), §155.

### Francis (1936–2025)

Pope from 2013 to 2025. The most-cited single source in *Magnifica Humanitas*. The encyclical's continuity with Francis's social teaching is structural. Key works:
- *Evangelii Gaudium* (2013) — apostolic exhortation; cited for the social dimension of evangelization
- *Laudato Si'* (2015) — on integral ecology; the source of the "technocratic paradigm" framing used extensively in Chapter 3
- *Fratelli Tutti* (2020) — on social friendship and universal fraternity; civilization of love revived
- *Dilexit Nos* (2024) — on the Sacred Heart, the integration of social commitment with the love of Christ

### Recent Church documents

*Dignitas Infinita* (2024) — Declaration of the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith on the infinite dignity of every person; cited in §53.

*Antiqua et Nova* (2025) — Note of the DDF and the Dicastery for Culture and Education on AI; cited extensively in Chapter 3 as the technical-philosophical companion to the encyclical.

*Quo vadis, humanitas?* (2026) — Document of the International Theological Commission; cited for the transhumanism/posthumanism analysis.

*Compendium of the Social Doctrine of the Church* (2004) — Cited for definitions of each principle.

## Patristic and scholastic sources

### Augustine (354–430)

Bishop of Hippo, Doctor of the Church. The encyclical's central theological frame in §130 — "two cities and two loves" — is Augustine's, from *The City of God*. The encyclical also cites Augustine in §11 (the restless heart from *Confessions*) and in §215, §234 (on Eucharistic unity and the sacrament of the body of Christ).

Augustine matters for the audit because the framing of two cities — the love of God and neighbor vs. the exclusive love of self — is the moral underlay of the Babel/Jerusalem distinction the encyclical uses throughout. A system that builds toward Babel is, in Augustine's framing, organized around the love of self even to the contempt of God; a system that builds toward rebuilt Jerusalem is organized around the love of God and neighbor.

### Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)

Dominican friar, Doctor of the Church, the central scholastic theologian. Cited in §127–128 on the theological framing of grace — the authentic "more than human" of Christian humanism, against transhumanism's technical "more than human." Aquinas's claim that grace surpasses every capability of created nature grounds the encyclical's contrast between technological self-transcendence (false) and divine self-transcendence through grace (true).

### Saint Benedict of Nursia (c. 480–547)

Founder of Western monasticism. The Rule of St. Benedict united prayer and work (*ora et labora*) — a unity the encyclical evokes in §148 as a foundation for understanding the dignity of work.

## Modern figures cited in the body of the encyclical

These are the persons cited explicitly in the body of *Magnifica Humanitas*, often as the encyclical's interlocutors on specific points.

### Romano Guardini (1885–1968)

Italo-German Catholic philosopher and theologian, one of the most influential Catholic thinkers of the twentieth century, an important influence on Joseph Ratzinger. Cited in §93 from *The End of the Modern World* (1950): "Contemporary man has not been trained to use power well."

This citation appears in Chapter 3's analysis of the technocratic paradigm. Guardini's thought addresses the moral disorientation produced by the rapid expansion of technical power without commensurate growth in human formation. His framing — that the problem of modern technology is not the technology itself but our unpreparedness for the power it gives us — is operative throughout the encyclical's treatment of AI.

For the audit: when a finding turns on the gap between technical capability and human formation, Guardini is the named authority.

### Viktor Frankl (1905–1997)

Austrian psychiatrist, Holocaust survivor, founder of logotherapy. Author of *Man's Search for Meaning* (1946). Cited in §121 in the context of the encyclical's discussion of human limitations and their integration into meaning. Frankl's line — that humanity is the being who invented the gas chambers of Auschwitz and also the being who entered them with the Lord's Prayer or the Shema on their lips — is cited as evidence that human dignity persists even in extremis, and that the integration of suffering (rather than its denial) is part of what makes us human.

For the audit: Frankl matters when findings engage transhumanism's promise to eliminate suffering, fragility, or limitation. The encyclical's position — that suffering integrated is part of dignity, not its enemy — is grounded here.

### Hannah Arendt (1906–1975)

German-American political theorist, Jewish refugee from Nazi Germany. Author of *The Origins of Totalitarianism* (1951). Cited in §134 on totalitarianism's ideal subjects: "people for whom the distinction between fact and fiction (i.e., the reality of experience) and the distinction between true and false (i.e., the standards of thought) no longer exist."

For the audit: Arendt is cited in the encyclical's treatment of truth as common good. When findings engage disinformation amplification, the erosion of shared fact, or algorithmic conditions that make truth-claims feel optional, Arendt's framing is the named diagnostic.

### Plato (c. 428–c. 348 BC)

Athenian philosopher. Cited in §140 from *Letter VII* on the slow nature of real understanding: "the deepest and most important things are learned only after much time and effort, by engaging in discussion with others, 'striking upon' ideas and experiences together like flint until the spark of understanding is kindled within us."

For the audit: Plato is invoked against the framing of AI as instant epistemic access. When a finding engages the substitution of speed for understanding, the displacement of patient inquiry by frictionless answers, Plato's framing is the named authority.

### J.R.R. Tolkien (1892–1973)

English Catholic author of *The Lord of the Rings* and *The Silmarillion*. Cited in §213. The line is from *The Return of the King*, given to Gandalf: "It is not our part to master all the tides of the world, but to do what is in us for the succour of those years wherein we are set, uprooting the evil in the fields that we know, so that those who live after may have clean earth to till."

For the audit: Tolkien is cited in the encyclical's framing of personal responsibility within constraints. The line frames the audit's own posture: we cannot resolve all the technological dilemmas of our era; we can speak honestly to the evil in the fields we can see.

### Giorgio La Pira (1904–1977)

Italian Catholic politician, mayor of Florence, peacemaker, candidate for canonization. Cited in §221: "the method of war be replaced by the method of peace: the method of negotiation, of encounter, of convergence, that is, the authentically human method!"

For the audit: La Pira is invoked in the encyclical's Chapter 5 discussion of replacing the "culture of power" with the "culture of negotiation." When findings engage AI in warfare, weapons systems, or adversarial systems, La Pira's framing of dialogue as the human method is the named alternative.

## A note on the audit's use of citations

The audit cites these figures only as the encyclical itself cites them. The encyclical is the authority. Guardini, Frankl, Arendt, Plato, Tolkien, and La Pira are present in the audit because Pope Leo XIV invokes them, not because the audit independently brings them in.

When writing findings, cite the encyclical paragraph that contains the reference. For example: "Per *Magnifica Humanitas* §134, citing Hannah Arendt, the ideal subjects of totalitarianism are those for whom 'the distinction between fact and fiction... no longer exist[s].'" The encyclical paragraph is the citation; Arendt is named for context.
