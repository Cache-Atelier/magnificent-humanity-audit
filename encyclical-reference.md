# *Magnifica Humanitas*: Reference for the Audit

This file gives the agent operational access to the encyclical's text and structure for the purposes of citation during audits. It is organized so paragraphs can be located quickly by topic. The full encyclical is available at https://www.vatican.va/content/leo-xiv/en/encyclicals/documents/20260515-magnifica-humanitas.html.

## The encyclical at a glance

*Magnifica Humanitas* ("Magnificent Humanity") was promulgated by Pope Leo XIV on 15 May 2026, the 135th anniversary of *Rerum Novarum*. Its full title is *On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence*. It consists of 245 numbered paragraphs in five chapters plus introduction and conclusion.

The encyclical's central biblical image is the contrast between the Tower of Babel (Gen 11:1–9) and the rebuilding of the walls of Jerusalem under Nehemiah (Neh 2–6). Babel is the construction conceived without reference to God, sacrificing human dignity for efficiency, aspiring to dominance. Jerusalem rebuilt is the work of shared responsibility, with God at the center, that rebuilds relationships before stones. The encyclical asks of every digital construction: which are we building?

The encyclical's litmus question, applied to AI and every emerging technology, is taken from John Paul II's *Redemptor Hominis* and repeated in §129: does it "make human life on earth 'more human' in every aspect of that life? Does it make it more worthy of man?"

## Chapter-by-chapter overview

**Introduction (§1–16).** The two biblical images. The *res novae* of our time. Four conditions for building the common good: a firm relationship with God; accepting the limits and weakness of humanity; shared responsibility; an evangelical language. Concludes: "remaining profoundly human."

**Chapter One: A Dynamic Approach Faithful to the Gospel (§17–45).** The Church's mode of presence in history. The development of Social Doctrine from Leo XIII through Francis. Sets up the seven principles as the inheritance the encyclical brings to bear on AI.

**Chapter Two: Foundations and Principles of the Social Doctrine of the Church (§46–89).** The principles themselves. *This is the chapter most directly relevant to the audit.* Foundations: human dignity, equal dignity of all, supreme value of human rights. Principles: common good, universal destination of goods, subsidiarity, solidarity, social justice. Integral human development as the framework for all. Concludes with the examen for the Church itself.

**Chapter Three: Technology and Dominance. The Grandeur of Humanity in Light of the Promises of AI (§90–130).** The technocratic paradigm. Artificial intelligence — what it is, what it isn't (does not have a body, feelings, conscience, moral judgment, lived experience). AI as a valuable tool requiring vigilance. Responsibility, transparency, governance. What must not be lost. Transhumanism and posthumanism critiqued. The "more than human" of grace. Augustine's two cities and two loves.

**Chapter Four: Safeguarding Humanity at a Time of Transformation. Truth, Work, Freedom (§131–181).** Truth as common good. Communication and disinformation. Education in the digital age. Dignity of work in digital transition. AI and unemployment. Economy that values dignity. Families and young people. Freedom against dependencies and digital attention economy. New forms of slavery in supply chains and trafficking. Shared responsibility.

**Chapter Five: The Culture of Power and the Civilization of Love (§182–228).** AI and war. Autonomous weapons and the ethical constraints required. The normalization of war. Crisis of multilateralism. False realism. Building the civilization of love through disarming words, building peace through justice, adopting the perspective of victims, healthy realism, dialogue, diplomacy. Praying and hoping.

**Conclusion (§229–245).** A program of Christian life: the Word became flesh; one body in Christ; the construction site of our time (fidelity to truth, education, relationships, justice and peace); the song of hope, Mary's *Magnificat*.

### On technology in general

**§4** — "Technology should not be considered, in itself, as a force antagonistic to humanity." Technology is "a profoundly human reality" but each phase reveals the ambiguity of tools that can cause harm when not oriented toward the good. "Never has humanity had such power over itself" (citing *Laudato Si'* §104).

**§9** — *Foundational for the audit.* "Technology is never neutral, because it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, regulate and use it." The choice is not "yes" or "no" to technology but between Babel and rebuilt Jerusalem.

**§10** — The "Babel syndrome": "the idolatry of profit that sacrifices the weak, a uniformity that neutralizes differences, and the pretense that a single language — even a digital one — can translate everything, including the mystery of the person, into data and performance."

**§85** — *The litmus question for any technology.* "Do they truly help individuals and peoples to become more humane and fraternal, while respecting our common home and future generations?"

### On the human person and dignity

**§48–50** — The human person is created in the image of the Triune God. Dignity does not depend on a person's abilities, wealth, position, or right or wrong choices made.

**§51** — *Often-cited for findings about productivity-grading.* "I consider particularly insidious the ideology that suggests that every person must earn or justify his or her own worth, to the point of attributing greater value to those who are more efficient or effective. From this perspective, persons end up being reduced to a means of achieving results, a resource to be used and exploited."

**§52–53** — Four dimensions of dignity (moral, social, existential, ontological). Per *Dignitas Infinita*: "Every human person possesses an infinite dignity, inalienably grounded in his or her very being, which prevails in and beyond every circumstance."

**§55** — Human rights are "inherent in the human person and in human dignity." Universal and inalienable. The first right is the right to life from conception to natural end.

### On the common good

**§59–60** — The common good is "the sum total of social conditions which allow people, either as groups or as individuals, to reach their fulfillment more fully and more easily." It is not the sum of individual benefits.

**§61** — "The mere sum of individual interests is not capable of generating a better world for the whole human family." The common good is a "plus" that transcends individual goods.

**§63** — When politics reduces to short-term calculations or sterile polarizations, "the language of the common good loses credibility, and, at the same time, social inequalities and divisions grow."

### On the universal destination of goods

**§65** — "God gave the earth to the whole human race for the sustenance of all its members, without excluding or favoring anyone." "It is not in accordance with God's plan to use this gift in such a way that its benefits accrue solely to a select few."

**§66** — Private property is subordinate to the universal destination of goods. "The Christian tradition has never recognized the right to private property as absolute or inviolable."

**§67** — *Foundational for data-and-algorithms findings.* "Today, among the goods that are universally intended for everyone, we must also include new forms of property, such as patents, algorithms, digital platforms, technological infrastructure and data... when these goods remain concentrated in the hands of a few, without adequate forms of sharing and access, a new imbalance is created that contradicts the universal destination of goods."

### On subsidiarity

**§68–69** — Higher-level institutions must recognize, protect, and promote the freedom and creativity of lower-level entities, coordinating their contributions rather than supplanting them.

**§70** — Decisions are made at the closest level possible to the persons involved, "thereby fostering community life and avoiding people being presented with decisions that have already been taken."

**§71** — *Foundational for platform-power findings.* "The principle of subsidiarity applies especially in the context of the digital revolution. Here, the highest level is not the State, but rather major economic and technological actors that exercise *de facto* power over the conditions of everyday life. This level, which monopolizes expertise, data and decision-making authority, involves companies and platforms that define conditions for access, rules of visibility, forms of interaction, and even economic opportunities. The principle of subsidiarity requires that such processes not be imposed from above in an opaque and unilateral manner, but instead be directed toward the common good with transparency, accountability and meaningful forms of participation (including independent checks, transparency regarding algorithms, equitable access to data and avenues for recourse)."

**§72** — "We cannot allow a handful of actors to dictate these processes on their own."

### On solidarity

**§73–74** — Solidarity is the conscious recognition that the future of each individual is connected to the future of all. "No one is saved alone." Digital networks already produce "de facto solidarity" — but it becomes solidarity in the full sense only when chosen.

**§75** — Solidarity as both principle and virtue: "a firm and persevering determination" to strive for the common good. Requires willingness to forego immediate benefits in order to create opportunities for others, including those tied to digital consumption and technology use.

**§76** — *Foundational for data-ecosystem findings.* "Like the natural environment, the 'digital ecosystem' can be preserved or exploited, shared or monopolized. Solidarity demands that decisions regarding data, algorithms, platforms and artificial intelligence take into account not only the immediate benefit for a few, but also the impact on all peoples and on future generations."

### On social justice

**§77–78** — Social justice begins with the least among us. The preferential option for the poor must guide both personal and societal choices.

**§79** — "Structures of sin" — injustices arising not only from individual choices but from structures and mechanisms that produce inequality almost automatically. Justice has a restorative dimension.

**§80** — *Foundational for algorithm-discrimination findings.* "Justice demands that we prevent the emergence of new forms of exclusion and deprivation of freedoms: individuals and peoples hindered or denied access to basic technologies, communities exposed to invasive surveillance and social groups penalized by opaque algorithms that perpetuate prejudice and discrimination. In the digital age, a just social order guarantees everyone equal access to opportunities, protects the youngest and weakest members of society, combats hate and misinformation and subjects the use of data and technology to public oversight, so that the guiding principle is not solely profit but the dignity of every person and the common good of all people."

### On integral human development

**§82–84** — Development is integral when it is not limited to the economic sphere but promotes quality of life in its spiritual, cultural, moral, and relational dimensions. Development is not human if it increases consumption for some while shifting costs onto others.

**§85** — The litmus question, cited above. "Technological innovations, including artificial intelligence, are not neutral, for they can either foster participation and justice or exacerbate inequality, control and exclusion."

### On the technocratic paradigm

**§92** — "The tendency to let the logic of efficiency, control and profit alone shape personal, social and economic decisions. This makes it clear that technology is not simply a tool. When it becomes the standard by which everything is judged, it begins to dictate what matters and what can be discarded, reducing creation to an object of exploitation and human beings to mere cogs in a system driven toward ever greater efficiency."

**§93** — Quotes Guardini: "Contemporary man has not been trained to use power well." More power does not necessarily mean better.

**§94** — Citing Paul VI: "The most extraordinary scientific progress, the most astounding technical feats and the most amazing economic growth, unless accompanied by authentic moral and social progress, will in the long run go against man." The result without ethical progress: "having more" without "being more." Persons evaluated by outcomes they produce.

**§95** — *On concentrated platform power.* Control over platforms, infrastructure, data and computing power rests not with States but with major economic and technological actors, who "set the conditions for access, determine the rules of visibility and shape the very possibilities for participation." When concentrated in a few hands, such power "tends to become opaque and evade public oversight," giving rise to new dependencies, exclusions, manipulations and inequalities. Pairs with §71 (subsidiarity) and §171 (architecture of visibility).

### On artificial intelligence specifically

**§98–99** — AI systems are "more cultivated than built." We know how they work less than we sometimes imagine. AI imitates functions of human intelligence and often surpasses it in speed, but does not undergo experience, possess a body, feel joy or pain, mature through relationships, or know love, work, friendship, or responsibility from within. AI does not have moral conscience.

**§100** — *Foundational for findings on individual AI use.* Three aspects requiring vigilance: ease of obtaining results (encouraging reliance, weakening creativity and judgment), impression of objectivity (concealing the cultural assumptions of designers), and simulation of human communication (creating the illusion of relationship with a personal subject). "When words are simulated, they do not build genuine relationships, but only their appearance... the danger is not so much that a person may believe they are communicating with another person, but rather that they may gradually lose the very desire to form genuine human connections."

**§101** — Environmental impact: AI requires enormous amounts of energy and water, significant carbon emissions, extensive infrastructure.

**§102** — *Foundational for findings on algorithmic decision-making.* "When AI systems present themselves as neutral and objective, they end up reflecting and reinforcing the stereotypes or ideological bias of their designers and developers." Algorithms making consequential decisions "do not know compassion, mercy, forgiveness, and above all, the hope that people are able to change."

**§103** — "Entrusting an algorithm in practice with the power to select who is worthy or not, without anyone bearing responsibility for that judgment, is to hand over the task of redefining the boundaries of human possibilities." Compassion, mercy, and forgiveness disappear from political action.

**§104** — *Foundational for the design-vs-use distinction.* "We cannot consider AI to be morally neutral. In reality, every technical tool embodies choices and priorities through what it measures, ignores and optimizes, and how it classifies people and situations. If a system is designed or used in a way that treats some lives as less worthy, or excludes them without the possibility of appeal, then it is not merely a tool 'to be used well,' since it has already introduced criteria that contradict the inalienable dignity of the human person."

**§105** — Accountability: who must "account" for decisions, justify them, monitor them, challenge them, and remedy any harm caused.

**§106** — Calling for prudence, rigorous evaluation, and at times a slower pace is not opposing progress. "It is not enough to invoke ethics in the abstract; robust legal frameworks, independent oversight, informed users and a political system that does not abdicate its responsibility are required."

**§108** — *Foundational for data-ownership findings.* "Ownership of data cannot be left solely in private hands but must be appropriately regulated. Data is the product of many contributors and should not be treated as something to be sold off or entrusted to a select few. It is necessary to think creatively in order to manage data as a common or shared good."

**§109** — Applies all five social-doctrine principles to AI: common good, universal destination of goods, subsidiarity, solidarity, and social justice. "Social justice is not only a goal to be safeguarded after technologies are deployed, but a condition that must shape their very design from the outset."

**§110** — *On "disarming" AI.* "To disarm AI means freeing it from the mentality of 'armed' competition, which today is not limited simply to the military context, but is also an economic and cognitive phenomenon. This entails a race for ever more powerful algorithms and larger datasets, driven by the desire to secure geopolitical or commercial dominance. To disarm means discrediting the assumption that technical power automatically confers the right to govern."

**§111** — *Direct address to developers.* "Developers, therefore, bear a particular ethical and spiritual responsibility, for every design choice reflects a vision of humanity. Just as the creator of an artistic or literary work must consider the values it conveys, so developers are called to embed values in their projects with due seriousness: with transparency, responsibility toward affected communities and careful attention to ensuring that what is being cultivated is a genuine good."

### On transhumanism and posthumanism

**§112** — The risk is not only misuse of certain technologies but the "anti-human vision" in which fullness of life is equated with "having more, reducing weakness, eliminating uncertainty and exerting total control. When efficiency becomes the ultimate measure of value, human beings are tempted to see themselves as a project to be optimized rather than as persons called to relationship and communion."

**§113** — Elevating any single dimension of human existence to an absolute is always a mistake. Disorder does not arise only from scarcity; even unchecked growth can give rise to impoverishment.

**§115–117** — Transhumanism: envisions human enhancement through technologies to increase performance and capabilities. Posthumanism: more radical, challenges anthropocentrism, envisions human-machine hybridization, anticipates a threshold where humanity "surpasses itself." Even when speculative, these gain relevance "by altering the collective imagination and thereby influence social, economic and political choices."

**§117** — *Key for findings against any "optimize the human" framing.* "If the human being is treated as something to be perfected or surpassed, it becomes easier to accept that some lives are less useful, less desirable or less worthy. In the name of progress, 'necessary sacrifices' may begin to be justified, placing the burden on the most vulnerable in pursuit of a supposed optimization of the species."

**§118–122** — Humanity flourishes not despite limitations but often through them. Suffering integrated rather than denied. Even painful expressions of limitation leave openings for the good. Cites Frankl on Auschwitz.

**§129** — *The Wojtyłan question, the climax of the chapter.* Does AI "make human life on earth 'more human' in every aspect of that life? Does it make it more worthy of man?" If yes, technology is to be embraced. If "power grows while the heart withers and human bonds fray, then we are faced with a new form of Babel."

**§130** — Augustine's two cities and two loves: "the love of God and neighbor" vs. "the exclusive love of self." "The construction of Babel or the rebuilding of Jerusalem begins within each one of us."

### On truth, disinformation, and communication

**§132** — *Foundational for findings on disinformation amplification.* Tools that could foster dialogue and participation are used to construct distorted narratives and blur boundaries between truth and falsehood. "The ability to manipulate content, images and videos exposes people to biased or misleading perspectives." Truth is rational (verification, sources, argumentation) and relational (trust, shared practices, honest exchange).

**§133** — Pure power detached from truth subtly imposes what it wishes others to accept as true. The "crisis of truth" goes deep: when universal truth about the good is lost, conscience itself changes.

**§134** — Cites Arendt on totalitarianism's ideal subjects: "people for whom the distinction between fact and fiction (i.e., the reality of experience) and the distinction between true and false (i.e., the standards of thought) no longer exist."

**§135–136** — Communication is the creation of a culture, not just transmission of information. What originates online becomes part of people's lives. Those who control platforms have considerable power to "affect the collective imagination and to present a particular vision of reality as desirable."

**§137** — *On the ecology of communication.* Public policy: transparent decision-making behind content selection, protection of personal data. Social and cultural: intermediary organizations, serious journalism, forums for debate. Families and schools: digital literacy, critical use of digital tools, AI, online commercial platforms.

### On education and the formation of persons

**§139–140** — Rapid technological transformations reveal how unprepared we are educationally. Digital media foster a culture of "immediacy and hyper-stimulation." Educating about AI "involves teaching them to decide when and for what purpose it ought *not* to be used." The speed and ease of AI-generated answers "risk extinguishing the desire to ask questions."

**§141** — *Foundational for findings on children's products.* "Early and unsupervised exposure to digital devices and social media can negatively impact sleep, attention span, control of emotions and relationships, especially during the most vulnerable stages of life, at times with tragic consequences." Easy access to violent, degrading, pornographic, or hypersexualized content. Grooming, blackmail, sexual exploitation of minors. AI tools that can manipulate images and videos.

**§142** — Public policies must oppose immediate interests of platforms when they conflict with the wellbeing of minors. Age limits on services, holding providers accountable rather than shifting burden to families, protections against sexual exploitation.

### On the dignity of work

**§148** — Work is "the essential key" to the social question. Created in the image of the Creator, human work continues God's creative work.

**§149** — Work is not simply an instrument; it expresses and enhances dignity. Financial assistance to the poor is necessary in emergencies but cannot be the sole response; the goal is enabling persons to live with dignity through their own work.

**§150** — *Foundational for findings on AI-driven worker surveillance and deskilling.* "While AI promises to boost productivity by taking over mundane tasks, it frequently forces workers to adapt to the speed and demands of machines, rather than machines being designed to support those who work." Current approaches "can paradoxically de-skill workers, subject them to automated surveillance and relegate them to rigid and repetitive tasks. The need to keep up with the pace of technology can erode workers' sense of agency."

**§151–155** — Unemployment as grave evil. Wealthy societies automating chaotically. Vast regions trapped in hybrid economies with underpaid human labor. Access to work as a high priority for public policy.

**§156** — Social criteria for innovation: every introduction of automation and AI accompanied by verifiable measures to protect employment, retraining, participation. Continuous training. Corporate commitment to quality and dignity of work as indicators of success.

### On freedom against dependencies

**§170** — *Foundational for findings on attention-economy mechanics.* "The subtler forms of addiction linked to the 'digital attention economy' should not be underestimated, since platforms and services are often designed to capture users' time and attention, exploiting their vulnerabilities and weakening their inner freedom. When business models thrive on human weakness, the person is treated as a means rather than as an end; those who design or finance such systems bear a moral responsibility that cannot be ignored."

**§171** — *Foundational for findings on profiling and behavioral architecture.* "Social control made possible by the massive collection of data and use of algorithmic systems. When every action—movements, purchases, relationships and preferences—leaves a trace, a new form of power emerges, namely the power to profile, predict and influence behavior, often without individuals being fully aware of it." Control through the architecture of visibility: what is amplified or rendered invisible, what is rewarded or penalized.

**§172** — Technocratic and post-humanist mentality regards the human person "as an object to be manipulated or a resource to be optimized."

### On new forms of slavery

**§173** — *Foundational for supply-chain findings.* "A significant part of the digital economy's functioning relies on the silent work of millions of people engaged in essential yet largely unseen activities, such as data labeling, model training and content moderation, often involving disturbing material. In many cases, these workers are young people, predominantly women, working under demanding conditions for minimal wages. Added to this invisible labor is the even harsher work of extracting the resources required for the production of the devices and microprocessors on which AI depends. In some regions of the world, children and adolescents work in dangerous conditions, crushing the materials from which rare earth elements are extracted."

**§174–175** — Renewed condemnation of all forms of slavery, trafficking, commodification of persons. Trafficking is contemporary slavery. Tolerating these practices makes us complicit.

**§178** — *On digital colonialism.* "Colonialism assumes new forms. It no longer dominates only bodies, but appropriates data, transforming personal lives into exploitable information. Entire regions, especially those marked by structural fragility and limited geopolitical relevance, are currently subjected to a new mindset of extraction: that of health data, epidemiological profiles, genetic maps and demographic information."

**§179** — Supply chains need transparency. Companies and investors need ethical verification criteria (due diligence). Digital platforms must cooperate with authorities and civil society to prevent communication, payment, and profiling tools from becoming channels for trafficking.

### On AI in warfare

**§183** — Hybrid warfare: cyberattacks, information manipulation, automation of strategic decisions. AI as accelerating factor. What is created for defense can be repurposed for offense.

**§197–200** — Autonomous weapons systems: their growing ease violates the principle that armed force should be used only as last resort. AI in warfare requires "the most rigorous ethical constraints." No algorithm can make war morally acceptable. Three criteria: personal responsibility; moral timeframe; identification and protection of civilians. Non-negotiable requirements: traceability of decisions, no delegation of lethal force to opaque processes, international framework to curb the technology arms race.

## How to cite

In findings, cite paragraphs in this form: "*Magnifica Humanitas* §67" or "Magnifica Humanitas §170–172" for ranges. Italics on the title where formatting allows; otherwise plain text. Direct quotations of fifteen words or fewer can be included in the finding; longer quotations should be summarized and the paragraph cited.

Multiple principles often apply to a single finding. Cite the primary principle in the main violation statement and any secondary principles as additional references. The seven principles are not isolated; *Magnifica Humanitas* §46 explicitly insists they "be considered collectively, so that it becomes clear how they relate to and complement each other."
